App 1 – Lifetime Mortgages

1. Introduction

FPG is authorised by the FCA for regulated residential mortgages and consumer buy to let mortgages, non-investment insurance contracts (including general insurance) and certain consumer credit activities.

The Mortgage Advice Standards reflect the requirements of the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and Finance Planning Group (FPG) for advising on mortgage business as set out in the Mortgage Conduct of Business Sourcebook (MCOB), Mortgage Credit Directive (MCD), Consumer Credit Sourcebook (CONC) and the principles of Treating Customers Fairly (TCF).

Advising and/or arranging sales of regulated mortgages and consumer credit agreements are regulated activities which have specific definitions and require the appropriate FCA permissions. Arranging is a broad term and can include introducing, referring, transacting on an execution only basis, or simply assisting a customer to complete an application form.

If you engage in a regulated activity and do not have the correct permissions, you will be breaching the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). It is therefore important that you fully understand what you can and cannot do.

As your Principal, FPG has FCA permissions for a number of regulated activities and via our contract with your AR Firm, we delegate some of these permissions to you, via these Mortgage Advice Standards. Detailed within these standards are the products that you can and cannot give advice or make a recommendation in, and those which you are permitted to refer. You must familiarise yourself with these details as if you go outside of the delegated permissions then you will breach either FCA and/or FPG rules.

These standards provide guidance and minimum requirements on what FPG expects from AR Firms when dealing with customers. They are not exhaustive and should be tailored to individuals considering their financial awareness.

You must always act in the customer's best interests. APERs or Senior Managers within your firm have a regulatory responsibility to ensure that you follow these standards. Where no specific ruling or guidance exists within these advice standards, we expect that an AR Firm, or one of its Advisers, will take a pragmatic view that is in the best interest of the customer, and not seek to exploit any apparent omissions.

If you intend transacting mortgages that are individually negotiated, e.g. from private banks and not sourced via the usual channels, you should contact Pete Burgess in the Compliance Department prior to the submission of the mortgage.

The key rule books that are relevant to the regulated business undertaken in AR Firms are:

  • MCOB for residential and consumer buy to let mortgages under the Mortgage Credit Directive (MCD)

CONC for Consumer Credit activities

ICOBs for non-investment insurance contracts including general insurance

2. Permitted Activities

& GENERAL GUIDANCE.

2.1 Definition of a regulated mortgage contract (RMC):

A contract which meets the following conditions:

A lender provides credit to an individual or trustees (the ‘borrower’), and

The obligation of the borrower to repay is secured by a mortgage on land in the EEA, and

At least 40% of which is used, or is intended to be used, as or in connection with a dwelling by an individual (or, where trustees are the borrower, by an individual who is a beneficiary of the trust) or by a related person

2.1.1 Related person definition:

A related person is defined in relation to a borrower or, in the case of credit provided to trustees, a beneficiary of the trust, as:

The borrower’s/ beneficiary’s spouse or civil partner,

A person (whether or not of the opposite sex) whose relationship with the borrower/ beneficiary has the characteristics of the relationship between husband and wife, or

The borrower’s/ beneficiary’s parent, brother, sister, child (including stepchildren and adopted children), grandparent or grandchild

2.1.2 Occupancy requirement

Within the definition of a regulated mortgage, the most obvious example is a loan made to an individual to enable them to buy a home for themselves where the loan is secured on that home. However, there is no requirement that the borrower should occupy the property. There is a requirement that at least 40% of the land should be used as a house but no requirement that it is the borrower who uses it as a house. So, for example:

A loan may be regulated if the property is to be occupied by the borrower’s relatives as their home (see the definition of a related person)

A loan may be regulated if the borrower does not occupy the property and instead intends to sell the property to a third party, with the mortgage remaining on the house until then

A loan may be regulated if made to a trustee acting for a large commercial company

2.2 Consumer buy to let (CBTL) mortgage contracts

The following are classed as CBTL agreements and are regulated under the MCD:

A buy to let credit agreement which is not entered into by the borrower for the purposes of a business, or

The borrower or a related person has lived in the property or intends to live in the property in the future

2.3 Products not regulated by the FCA:

Variations made to mortgage contracts entered into before 31 October 2004, if there is no increase in the original loan amount (unless the variation results in the original loan being replaced).

Overseas mortgages on land outside the EEA.

Business buy to let (BBTL) mortgages that are to be occupied as a dwelling on the basis of a rental agreement and have not/will not at any time be occupied as a dwelling by the borrower, beneficiary of a trust or by a related person.

2.4 Business buy to let (BBTL) credit agreements

Since the implementation of MCD, BBTL mortgages have been taken out of regulation.

Whilst FCA rules do not cover the advice and sales process you adopt in relation to non-regulated BBTLs, professional indemnity insurance cover is in place and a Point of Sale (POS) record must be retained. You must apply the same standards and sales practices as you would for regulated business.

In order to be classified as a non-regulated BBTL mortgage, the requirements are that the land, or existing or projected building (as applicable):

Cannot at any time be occupied as a dwelling by the borrower or by a related person; and

Is to be occupied as a dwelling on the basis of a rental agreement

In the above cases, the mortgage will be deemed to be a BBTL mortgage unless any person acting on behalf of the creditor (e.g. lender, adviser, surveyor, solicitor/ conveyancer) knows or has reasonable cause to suspect that the agreement is not entered into by the borrower wholly or predominantly for the purpose of a BBTL.

2.5 Business buy to let (BBTL) credit agreements with debt consolidation

Where a client wishes to consolidate debt onto a BBTL loan, the credit agreement will be regulated under CONC.

2.6 Foreign currency loans

The definition of a foreign currency loan is one where the credit is denominated in a currency other than that in which the customer receives the income or holds the assets from which the credit is to be repaid.

For example:

Customer A – who is resident in the UK, employed by an American company and paid in US dollars. He purchases a UK property, with a part and part mortgage. He intends to repay the interest only mortgage by the sale of shares he holds in his company, which is listed on the American stock exchange.

Foreign currency lenders will need to ensure that the customer has a right to convert the mortgage into an alternative currency under specified conditions or, alternatively, that there are other arrangements in place to limit the exchange rate risk to which the customer is exposed under the mortgage. For example, a cap or a risk warning to limit the exchange rate risk to which the customer is exposed.

The above should be included within the mortgage illustration and offer letter.

2.7 POS (Point of Sale) record

The POS record must make it clear whether the case is a regulated or non-regulated mortgage.

If you are unsure as to whether the loan will be regulated or not, you should contact the Compliance Department before you contract the business.

2.8 Permitted activities

2.8.1 FCA MCOB regulated products

All residential mortgages, including further advances and consumer buy to let mortgages, where they satisfy the definition conditions in Section 2.1. This includes:

Consumer buy to let mortgages, which have been used, or will be used, as a dwelling by the borrower e.g. let to buy mortgages.

Commercial mortgages, secured on commercial property, subject to the definition conditions.

Business loans secured against a residential property, subject to the definition conditions, and the business is not a large business (group annual turnover of less than £1m).

MCD exempt lifetime mortgages, where customers release equity from their main residence and the lender does not require repayment of the capital until the occurrence of one or more of the specified life events (when the customer dies, moves into long term residential care, moves their main residence, sells the property or the lender repossesses the property).

Foreign currency loans, where an agreement is: (a) denominated in a currency other than that in which the customer receives the income or holds the assets from which the credit is to be repaid, or (b) denominated in a currency other than that of the EEA state in which the customer is resident

Mortgages for land in the UK or an EEA state, subject to the definition conditions

2.8.2 FCA CONC regulated products

Consumer credit activities listed in the table below are regulated by the FCA and relate to activities which are carried out in respect of consumer credit agreements. A consumer credit agreement is essentially any kind of loan, deferment of repayment of any loan or interest on any loan, and any other kind of accommodation or facility in the nature of credit.

This includes: hire purchase agreements, pay day loans, bridging loans not regulated under MCOB and insurance products with a home premium finance element where annual premiums can be deferred and paid monthly.

2.9 Mortgage panels

2.9.1 Panel usage

FPG operates and offers your AR Firm a panel of lenders representative of the market. When selecting products from the panel, advisers must provide clear evidence that the product selected and recommended is the cheapest from those most suitable to meet your customers’ requirements.

Whilst a panel of lenders is in place, your AR Firm will be able to recommend products from ‘off-panel’ lenders to provide the most suitable advice to customers, subject to justification in terms of price, value and suitability.

If you intend transacting mortgages that are individually negotiated (e.g. not sourced via usual channels) you must contact pete.burgess@financeplanning.co.uk in the first instance for approval.

2.9.2 Purpose of the panel

MCOB rules state that authorised firms must ensure the product selection is appropriate to a customer’s needs and circumstances on the facts disclosed by the customer.

FPG ensures that its panel caters for the vast majority of your AR Firm’s regulated residential and buy to let mortgage contract requirements. The panel also:

Acts as a benchmark to assist Advisers in making recommendations in line with FPG’s expectations

Provides useful indications of where more focused risk monitoring might be required for transactions both on and off panel

Reflects factors relevant to advice criteria which are not indicated by sourcing systems, e.g. service standards, underwriting

2.10 Packagers

An approved packager panel has been developed to assist you in the administration of mortgage cases and the placement of cases that do not fit the product range of FPG’s comprehensive proposition. Only FPG approved packagers can be used, details of those can be found on the FPG’s Knowledge Hub website, or L&G’s Mortgage Club website.

The approved panel provides your AR Firm with the flexibility to select a packager that can provide a product which best fits the needs of the client, and gives you the confidence of dealing with creditable packagers.

The appointment of these packagers is based around existing relationships, but new packagers can be appointed to the panel subject to credit standing, quality of service, range of lenders and geographical cover. All packagers will need to successfully pass a stringent application process and reach the minimum acceptance criteria defined by FPG. The panel is reviewed on an ongoing basis.

2.10.1 Pure packagers

The pure packagers provide an administration only service. They are not authorised to give advice to or have contact with the client. The advice given to the client, when using a pure packager, remains the full responsibility of you and your AR Firm.

2.11 Hand-Offs and Referrals

FPG has negotiated terms with certain third party providers with whom we have existing relationships, or where we have undertaken due diligence to be satisfied that these providers have controls in place to offer a professional and reliable service.

  • FPG has in place panels for the following products:
  • Second charge mortgages, secured loans and unsecured loans
  • All bridging finance
  • Commercial mortgages
  • You cannot transact the above business and you are only permitted to refer to the approved FPG providers.
  • The following process must be adhered to in all cases:
  • Obtain your customer’s consent to pass their details to a third party
  • Explain what service you are referring the customer to and why, and that you can’t/don’t offer advice in this area.

Disclose that you will receive a fee from the provider as a result of the referral

Confirm that the provider, not you, will be responsible for any advice or information they provide

Provide the client with a letter confirming the above

Any letters you issue must be attached to the POS system record and issued to the customer at the point of referral.

It is important that this process is followed because whilst you may not be responsible for advice or information other providers may give, unless your customer is aware of the basis of referral they may not understand this and in the event of a complaint, believe you are responsible as you referred them.

2.12 Decision in Principle (DIP) / Application in Principle (AIP)

Where a customer has no particular property in mind to purchase, and you don’t anticipate giving advice or a recommendation until they are in a better position to proceed, you can give an indication of their borrowing capacity by using a website calculator or tool. Alternatively, you can provide a lender DIP/AIP. Note: DIPs and AIPs are considered part of the formal advice process and you are required by the FCA to retain a record of all advice.

Providing a DIP which steers a customer towards a particular lender or product will be seen as advice by the FCA. Therefore, for customers with no specific property in mind or completion is some time off e.g. new build:

Create a POS record with the customer’s name and details required for the DIP, as a minimum.

Source for a suitable lender (you should be reasonably certain that the customer will meet the chosen lender’s criteria).

Inform the customer of the implications of using this facility, e.g. credit scoring and footprint.

Get the customer’s explicit agreement to proceed with the DIP.

Read to the customer the specific lender’s Data Protection/Use of information disclaimer.

Attach a copy of the DIP/AIP to the customer record.

Ensure the customer is aware that this is not a recommendation to use this lender or its products but only an indication of borrowing.

Advise them that you will re-source from the whole of market when they find a suitable property to ensure you recommend the most suitable lender/product at that time.

Where the customer has found a suitable property, you should follow the full sales and advice process, source and obtain the DIP from the lender you intend submitting the formal application to.

2.13 Submitting Business to Lenders

All DIPs, AIPs and applications must be completed and submitted by the adviser under their individual codes. Lender codes MUST NOT be shared under any circumstances.

If advisers rely on administrators and the lender’s system allows administrators to submit under a separate code on behalf of the adviser, this is acceptable. The adviser remains responsible for the advice and should check the application before submission. Where the lender does not allow separate administrator access, the adviser must submit the DIP/AIP/application themselves.

An application to secure a particular product can only be made when you have evidenced all the required paperwork that supports the case (bank statements, evidence of income, proof of address and identity).

2.14 Staying With The Existing Lender

In all cases, you must follow the full sales process, including sourcing the whole of market, to ensure that the existing lender’s product is the best available, as well as demonstrating suitability and affordability. The only exception to this is where the client is insistent on staying with their present lender and in such circumstances you will need to confirm to the client (in the MRWL) that the whole of market hasn’t been sourced because of their overriding preference.

2.15 Direct Products

You cannot give advice or make a recommendation on Direct products (which are offered to consumers by a bank directly).

You can only recommend the most suitable product from those available to you. You can suggest your customer approach a lender direct but you cannot steer them to any particular lender or product.

2.16 Execution only

FPG does not permit any mortgage business to be conducted on any basis other than fully advised. Execution only is not permitted.

2.17 Selling to yourself

If you wish to arrange a Residential mortgage, a CBTL mortgage or consolidate debt to a BBTL mortgage for yourself on a sole basis, you can either:

Go direct to the lender and transact the mortgage on an execution only basis as a client (no proc fee), or

Arrange the mortgage via another adviser within your AR Firm on a fully advised basis

For joint applications, you should arrange the mortgage via another adviser within the AR Firm on a fully advised basis. The reason for this is to ensure that the conflict between adviser and partner/spouse is managed, and for non-mortgage professional individuals the route to advice is offered.

2.18 Use of funds raised by a mortgage

FPG does not permit money to be released from a property to be placed into:

Investments (for any purpose other than buy to let purchases)

Defined investment schemes

Company schemes

Investment in land

Overseas land/ property where the customer will be reliant on income from the land/ property to repay the mortgage

2.19 Overseas Deposits

2.19.1 Overseas Deposits - guidance

Your customers may wish to purchase a property and pay the deposit from an overseas source. This may be a gift from a family member or from their own funds. Overseas being defined as any territory outside of the UK, Republic of Ireland, the Channel Islands and Isle of Man. This guidance is designed to help you make sure you have as much evidence of your due diligence as possible.

The mortgage lender takes the risk of accepting potentially illegitimate deposit payments but as the intermediary, you have a responsibility for the business you are placing with them. A mortgage lender is likely to hold you responsible for any business that they discover to be from an illegitimately sourced deposit.

It is important you are able to evidence this source of deposit to ensure, as best you can, that it is from a legitimate source:

  • Is there a clear link between the source of funds and your customer?
  • Based on what you know of the customer’s circumstances is the deposit amount plausible?
  • Have they built up the funds over time or have they arrived in a lump sum?

2.19.2 Customer’s Own Money – evidencing the deposit

With regard to evidence, if the deposit is the customer’s own, the following are good examples of what you can request:

Bank statements – preferably the funds have already entered the UK, therefore, by requesting copies of UK and overseas statements you can see trail the money has followed and clearly link it to the customer.

Investment statements – if the customer has stated they are liquidating investments obtaining these statements backs up this story.

Property sale – where the sale of a property abroad has provided the deposit, documentary evidence of this would be useful. Ensure the customer’s name is clear on any documents you obtain.

With regard to statements they should always clearly show the account holder’s name, address, sort code, account number and identify the bank or financial institution concerned. Print outs from ATM machines are not acceptable.

If after collecting the necessary information, you are comfortable with the situation, proceed as normal with the chosen lender. Only refer a case to our MLRO pete.burgess@financeplanning,co.uk if you have concerns over the customers’ story, following the normal Suspicious Activity Referral process.

2.19.3 Money gifted by a third party – gifted deposits

If the deposit is a gift or from a third party, further work is required:

Evidence the link between the customer and the person gifting the funds. This is often by way of a gifted deposit letter confirming the relationship and must also state their name, address and details of the property to be purchased to demonstrate they know what the money is for. It should also be as recent as possible i.e. within the preceding three months.

Most lenders require this money to be an unencumbered gift to ensure that the third party has no rights over the property. This should be confirmed in the letter but you should check with the lender first.

A copy of the third party’s bank / investment statement should be requested to show the funds in situ. As above, it is easier to see the audit trail if the funds have already been transferred into the UK so statements show the journey the funds have taken.

All details of any foreign accounts, related address and third parties (gifting parents / relatives etc.) should be recorded in the notes section on the POS system.

Provided that the above guidance is followed there is no requirement to refer the documentation to our MLRO, Pete Burgess, unless you have concerns over the customer’s story, in which case a Suspicious Activity Referral should be raised.

Finally, in every case, speak to the chosen lender directly and explain everything you are able to obtain. From a lender’s perspective, clarity and transparency are key.

If you have any queries please contact pete.burgess@financeplanning.co.uk for further information.

All mortgage offers are binding and they provide the customer with a seven day (minimum) reflection period. The customer has the right to waive the reflection period, but they must liaise with their solicitor where this is required.

2.21 Consumer Buy To Let (CBTL) Mortgages – General Guidance

Consumer buy to let (CBTL) mortgages became regulated on 21 March 2016.

These Mortgage Advice Standards apply to all CBTL mortgages, together with the additional requirements detailed below:

2.21.1 Let to buy (and accidental landlords / inherited property)

Where customers intend to buy another property in which to live and let out their existing property (known as let to buy), any mortgage on the current residential property will become a regulated CBTL mortgage. (See Section 2.2)

2.21.2 Customer information and risk warnings

When completing the fact find, you should use the “Buy To Let Factsheet” with all CBTL customers and give them a copy. You may wish to use the Factsheet with your BBTL customers too, but this is at your discretion and may depend on the financial awareness of the customer and familiarity with the buy to let process.

You should advise the customer to seek professional advice with regards to any tax implications on letting a property.

The customer also needs to know that if property prices fall, or if the property can’t be let profitably, this could prove very costly to them. These risks should be reiterated in the MRWL.

CBTL customers need to understand:

That the return may not be what they expected as house prices can fall in value

That they may need to fund the mortgage when there are no tenants

That deposits are higher

The tax implications for let properties differ from residential properties and you should advise those of your customers who are inexperienced in buy to let property investments, to seek professional advice. You should also direct them to the following websites, as they provide valuable information for landlords, including the tax implications for buy to let properties:

Government website - https://www.gov.uk/renting-out-a-property/paying-tax

Inland Revenue website - http://www.hmrc.gov.uk/cgt/property/reliefs.htm#2

National Landlords Association website - National Landlords Association

You should also advise your customers to seek expert guidance from an accountant.

2.21.3 Income

Proof of income must be obtained from all customers. Whilst this will not be required by all lenders, it is still a requirement of FPG. You must endeavour to obtain three month’s payslips and corresponding bank statements where possible, but always have a minimum of one month’s payslip and corresponding bank statement, or one year’s accounts, and you must assess the customer for affordability in the same way that you would for a residential mortgage, i.e. you must not proceed if the mortgage appears unaffordable.

You must also obtain evidence that any profits on existing BTL properties are declared for Land & Property Tax on their most recent tax return.

You should also recommend that customers set aside/build up an emergency fund to help pay the monthly mortgage payments in the event that their tenants fail to pay any/all rental income when due.

2.22 Shared Equity and Shared Ownership Schemes

2.22.1 Builders Incentive Schemes

Some builders offer initiatives called ‘shared equity schemes’ where the purchaser buys a percentage of the property by way of a mortgage and the builder takes a second charge on the remaining percentage.

The second charge becomes payable at the prevailing open market value within a specified period, e.g. 10 years or earlier upon the sale of the property, redemption of the mortgage, or death. Some schemes have interest free loans for the full period, whilst others have deferred interest for a number of years.

The MRWL should confirm the following:

  • How the customer will repay the second charge loan
  • That you have not provided advice on the second charge

2.22.2 Government Schemes

The Government have various low cost home ownership schemes to help individuals onto the property ladder. The advice standards apply to all schemes, which include:

Right to buy / acquire

Shared ownership

Help to Buy - equity loans, mortgage guarantees, etc

Where the lender recommended is not at the top of the sourcing list, you must add notes to clarify why e.g. the first two lenders do not lend on to customers borrowing on Government schemes to demonstrate that the recommended product is the most suitable / least expensive for the customer’s needs and preferences.

2.22.2 a For shared equity mortgages:

You must also discuss with the customer and document how they are going to repay the equity loan at the end of the specified loan period. You will also need to assess affordability when the deferred interest charge becomes payable in year six.

You should consider and discuss whether the customer can afford to purchase a property using their own resources, rather than by borrowing under a shared equity credit agreement. The result of this discussion must be included within the MRWL.

2.22.2 b For shared ownership mortgages:

The rental payment for shared ownership properties must be included in the expenditure section.

2.22.2 c For Help to Buy mortgage guarantee loans:

These should be treated as any other mortgage as the customer is responsible for the full amount of the loan.

2.22.3 Mandatory record keeping requirements for all schemes

For all of the above schemes, you must:

Document full details of the scheme within the POS record, including the amount of deferred interest that will become payable during the term and when.

Confirm in the MRWL how the mortgage will remain affordable when any deferred interest payments become due.

Discuss and document in the MRWL how the customer will repay the equity loan at the end of the specified loan period.

Include within the MRWL the appropriate risk warnings detailed in the template letter

You should also refer to the Protection Advice Standards, which require you to consider the customers’ protection needs for all liabilities directly linked to the mortgage, such as Builders Incentive Schemes and Government Schemes.

3. Initial Disclosure to Customers

3.1 General guidance for residential and all BTL mortgages:

If your discussion with a customer is about mortgages and the possibilities of obtaining a mortgage in general, then you must make them aware you are not giving specific recommendations at this stage, and that you will need to do a full review of their circumstances before advice can be given. You must make it clear to the client at what point advice is being given as opposed to generic information.

You must give the customer the “Disclosure Documentation” on first contact where you make, or anticipate making, a personal recommendation or where you give, or anticipate giving, personalised information to a customer.

You must fully explain all sections of the “Disclosure Documentation” to the customer:

If the mortgage falls through, or the customer decides to go direct to a lender, you will still need to review the customer’s protection arrangements as detailed in your “Disclosure Documentation” scope of service.

Where you have provided a “Disclosure Documentation” but don’t intend to provide protection advice until a later date e.g. when the mortgage offer has been received, you must add the appropriate paragraph to your MRWL. This same rule (adding an appropriate paragraph) applies where the client has told you that they specifically do not wish to review their protection needs as part of this process. As you have disclosed that you will be providing advice for both the mortgage and protection needs, you must ensure that protection advice has been given before exchange of contracts.

3.2 Express Consent & GDPR

The FCA does not allow cold calling for mortgages so before you contact any customer, you must have obtained permission via express consent (known as our GDPR form as it also covers off what we do with a customer’s personal information).

What is it?

It is the customer’s explicit permission to receive contact in respect of regulated MCOB / CONC mortgage advice.

Why do we need to get it?

The FCA enforces a ban on cold calling for mortgage business. Your file needs to show that you received consent to make contact, whether that contact be made by telephone, email or by referral (via estate agents or other referral processes). You will also need it for future contact, e.g. where your customer contacted you initially, you will need a signed GDPR form on file for any future contact with them.

Who should obtain this consent?

All introducers should get the consent verbally from the customer in order to pass a referral to you. You will then need to ask the customer to sign a GDPR form early in your sales process.

It is a breach of FCA rules to commence an appointment without verbal express consent. It is also your responsibility to maintain evidence of compliance with these requirements, by way of a signed GDPR form for your customer.

3.3 Additional Guidance for all telephone sales

All relevant advice standards for mortgage sales apply to telephone sales. Where you have a telephone based sales operation, you will need to ensure that the following high-level standards are also met:

You must have the customer’s express consent prior to contact.

All calls must be carried out in a way that is clear, fair and not misleading.

You must recognise and respect that the customer can end a call at any time and refuse another appointment.

You must not call at an unsocial hour or on an unlisted telephone number unless previously agreed.

You must give an idea of the length of time the call is likely to take at the start of your introduction.

If calls are recorded, you must inform the customer of this as early in the call as is feasible and reasonable to do so.

3.3.1 Telephone disclosure

When making the initial call you must explain the content of the “Disclosure Documentation”.

Once you have completed the call, and within three business days, the following must be issued via email or post, depending on how the customer wishes to receive the information:

The “Disclosure Documentation”

The fee agreement (if applicable)

Your Mortgage & Protection brochure (for First Time Buyers)

The Mortgage Illustration (if applicable)

4. Knowing Your Customer

4.1 General guidance for regulated mortgages

You should explain to your client the process that you will take them through and approximately how long each step will take. For example, from initial disclosure and fact finding through to completion of the mortgage, explaining the relevance of the information the customer provides (i.e. their credit history), the lending decision process, and the application and underwriting process.

You must complete a comprehensive fact find to enable you to know your customer, so you understand their circumstances, preferences, and needs. Your record should provide a clear picture of the customer’s current circumstances, as well as their needs and objectives, and should show how your recommendation meets those needs and objectives to provide the right outcome.

If you identify anything which might suggest either customer to be vulnerable, you must record this in the find find and then take reasonable steps to help mitigate any issues because of their vulnerability. Please refer to our separate Vulnerable Persons guidance notes.

You must stress to customers the importance of them providing full and accurate information in order that you can make a suitable recommendation. You must speak to all parties of the mortgage to confirm the accuracy of the information. Customers omitting to provide full information risk a lender identifying this and rejecting applications further down the process.

When completing the fact find, you must use the “Your Mortgage & Protection” brochure with your “first time buyer” customers and give them a copy. You may wish to use the brochure with other customers, but this is at your discretion and may depend on the financial awareness of the customer and familiarity with the mortgage process.

Where you carry out customer reviews, e.g. at rate expiration, you should check past records to refresh your memory on discussions held at previous meetings. For example, referring back to any debt consolidation or interest only repayment plans. If a fact find from a previous case is used for a new case, then it must be thoroughly checked and updated where applicable, to fully reflect the customer’s current circumstances.

Minimum requirements for fact find completion for all regulated mortgage contracts

4.2 Employment details

Full details must be included, and the record should be clear as to the customer’s employment status, for example employed (if part time, how many hours per week), self-employed (sole trader, partner, director and % shareholding should be fully explained in notes) or contract worker (length and type of contract, start and end dates).

4.3 Retirement age

You must document the customer’s intended retirement age and assess whether this is reasonable. For example, a police officer or scaffolder is unlikely to work to 70, whereas a self-employed solicitor or accountant may. If the customer is expecting to change employment from their current profession at retirement, you must detail this and consider any potential reduction in salary.

4.4 Liabilities

You must document full details of the customer’s liabilities (e.g. loans, credit cards, overdrafts) including whether there are any payment arrears or shortfalls. Payments made on liabilities should be included within the expenditure section. If any liabilities are being consolidated to the mortgage, the fact find and MRWL must reflect this.

4.5 Income

You must record full details of all income, including non-salaried income such as pension, investment income, state benefits, savings, or rental income etc. You must satisfy yourself that the income is reasonable and evidence this in all cases from original documents, attaching copies to the POS record.

The employer details shown on the bank statements should reflect that of the evidence provided (employer name and amount).

If the customer is self-employed, you must take tax and National Insurance into account when assessing their net income. For fact find and sourcing purposes, net profit equates to gross income. If self-employed customers are unable to produce accounts, you must still seek to obtain some form of documentation, for example, bank statements, plus tax returns, accountant’s letter etc.

If the customer can’t evidence the required income then you should assume they don’t earn it. You may also conclude this is suspicious, in which case it should be reported. You must submit a Suspicious Activity Referral (SAR) immediately and withdraw from the sale without tipping the customer off.

You must ask the customer whether they are aware of any expected changes to income or expenditure within the next five years (minimum) that are likely to affect their ability to meet the mortgage payments. These must be detailed in the fact find and MRWL. Uncertain changes cannot be taken into account, e.g. improved economic prospects or assumed pay increases.

You should validate the authenticity of the documents provided. FPG will provide guidance from time to time on fraud controls and you should ensure, via your Sales Manager, you are fully briefed on these to protect yourself.

4.6 Expenditure

The expenditure section must be fully completed to reflect anticipated personal outgoings after the mortgage completes (excluding the monthly mortgage payment). You must ensure these are realistic and check on-going liabilities against bank statements to ensure accuracy. If the bank statements do not detail the usual monthly expenditure as customers pay via credit cards, the latest credit card statement(s) will also be required. This includes credit card statements where the balance is paid in full every month.

Any payments made to savings or investment plans supporting the repayment of interest only residential mortgages must be reflected in expenditure.

For shared ownership mortgages, the rental payment must be included in expenditure. If the customer does not yet know the actual monthly payment, this is typically between 2.5% to 3% of the capital value of the unsold share, and this calculation can be used for affordability purposes.

Any expected changes to expenditure should be discussed and noted.

4.7 Existing mortgage details

Full details must be recorded, including the existing lender, repayment method, interest rate and type, amount outstanding, current monthly repayment amount, term remaining, current property value, any penalties payable, the penalty end date and details of any mortgage payment arrears or shortfalls.

Details of any buy to let properties must include number of properties, total current value, total outstanding mortgages, total rental income, and total monthly mortgage payments. This information can either be documented in notes or detailed on an attached spreadsheet.

If the client is responsible for any other mortgage or properties, details must be recorded in your fact find.

4.8 Mortgage deposit, requirements and preferences

The source of deposit must be recorded, and the feasibility should be assessed. Evidence of the deposit must be obtained and attached to the record. If evidence cannot be obtained then notes must be added explaining why, and consideration given to a SAR being reported.

Where you are advising a customer on a regulated or non-regulated mortgage and the source of deposit isn’t from a UK bank account then you must refer to Section 2.19 for guidance.

When discussing preferences with customers, you should probe and challenge these before designing your recommendation. Customers may have pre-determined preferences, such as a 2 year tracker rate or 25 year term, when alternative options may be more suitable to their needs and circumstances.

You must record the customer’s specific preferences and the reason for those preferences. You must make them aware that preferences for product features reduce the products available to them.

If customers prefer an initial benefit period, the reason for the length of that period must be recorded. You must not automatically assume the customer should have a two year rate because they want the lowest monthly payment without fully discussing the implications of continually re-mortgaging. For example, depending on market conditions, a five year rate might cost slightly more but taking into account the fees involved in re-mortgaging every two years, the longer benefit period could be a better outcome for the customer. Your MRWL should refer to your discussions around longer benefit periods and the implications to the customer.

The customer’s preference for the term of the mortgage should be fully discussed and recorded. Your discussions should include the impact of interest rate rises and whether the customer is disciplined enough to be able to make overpayments which can then be reduced if rates rise to ensure the monthly payment remains affordable. Whilst interest rates remain low, it may not provide the right customer outcome to recommend the shortest term.

You should not predict, nor ask customers to predict, future interest rates. You should ensure during discussions and fact finding with customers that the impact of rate rises is fully considered and noted.

You must not assume that any fees are to be added to the loan. You should discuss with your customer whether they intend to add fees to the loan or pay these upfront. Where your customer opts to add fees to the loan this positive opt in must be recorded in the fact find and the MRWL must explain the implications of interest charges.

4.9 Credit history

Full details of the customer’s credit history for a minimum of six years must be recorded. You should make your customer aware that that this will assist in the lending decision process and that where they fail to disclose, this will be identified and potentially result in the application being declined.

In some instances, you may find it prudent to advise the customer to obtain their own credit history report which you can use to assist with their application.

Some customers will be classed as ‘credit impaired’. The definition is a customer who:

Within the last two years has owed overdue payments, in an amount equivalent to three months’ payments, on a mortgage or other loan (whether secured or unsecured) except where the amount overdue reached that level because of late payment caused by errors by a bank or other third party; or

Has been the subject of one or more County Court Judgements, with a total value greater than £500, within the last three years; or

Has been subject to an individual voluntary arrangement or bankruptcy order which was in force at any time within the last three years

4.10 Debt Consolidation

If the customer wishes to consolidate debts and has consolidated before, you must document the full history, e.g. the purpose, amount and number of times (please use the fact find “notes” section).

Where the customer has previously consolidated debts to fund necessary bills, this suggests affordability issues and therefore it raises serious doubt for them to continue consolidating. There’s a clear difference between consolidating debts for a specific/one off purpose, e.g. home improvements, as opposed to funding lifestyle and necessary bills.

You should apply extreme caution where customers are consolidating debt and whose situation may indicate affordability issues that challenge the suitability, e.g. consolidating coupled with interest only and/or term into retirement.

Conversely, where the customers can comfortably afford their current debt commitments, you will need to establish a need for consolidation, and your record will need to reflect this. If the customer can afford to continue to pay the debts, they should not consolidate to the mortgage and should seek an alternative approach.

You should investigate whether it would be more suitable to consolidate to a loan NOT secured on their property, and then refer them as necessary to our panel of “2nd charge mortgage brokers”.

If you think it is unsuitable for the customer to consolidate, they should be referred to the Citizen’s Advice Bureau and you should not transact the business.

4.11 Repayment method

You should discuss whether a repayment, interest only or part repayment/interest only mortgage would be more suitable for your customer. Where customers do not have any existing credible repayment strategies that are acceptable to lenders, then a repayment mortgage would be the only suitable option.

Details of all interest only repayment strategies must be recorded in the fact find “notes” section.

All plans must be reiterated back to the customer, including why you have recommended interest only in the MRWL, plus any applicable risks.

You must always provide the customer with a comparable repayment illustration when recommending either interest only or a part repayment/interest only mortgage.

Savings should also be detailed within the fact find “notes” and considered when discussing preferences such as whether offset would be suitable.

4.12 Deposits

For purchase mortgages you must ensure that the customer has sufficient funds available to fund the deposit and associated moving costs. Evidence of these funds must be obtained and checked for plausibility. For instance, if the deposit has been saved up over a period of time, then you will need to see monthly contributions being added to the fund and be comfortable that this has been afforded from their disposable income, whereas if large sums have been added to the deposit account then you must establish where these monies have originated from.

With regard to evidencing the customer’s own deposit, the following is required

Bank statements – showing the money on deposit, and sufficient in period to satisfy you of how it has been accumulated,

Investment statements – if the customer has stated they are liquidating investments, obtaining these statements supports this story,

Property sale – where a previous sale of property is contributing towards the deposit, documentary evidence of this is required. Ensure the customer’s name is clear on any documents you obtain,

Inheritance – if the customer has inherited money, then a letter from the solicitor or executor of the estate should support these monies.

Please also refer to Section 2.19, and sub-sections, of this document.

If after collecting the necessary information, you are comfortable with the situation, proceed as normal with the chosen lender.

If the customer has told you that an element of the deposit is held in crypto-currency, then you must refer to case to our MLRO who will provide you with further guidance. As an indication of additional requirements, this will include statements showing the client money buying crypto-currency, as well as discussions with the proposed lender and conveyancer to check that they are comfortable with the source of funds.

Aside from crypto-currency deposits, if you have any concerns over the customers’ deposit scenario, please refer the customer to our MLRO pete.burgess@financeplanning,co.uk, following the normal Suspicious Activity Referral process.

For gifted deposits, please refer to Section 2.19.3 of these Advice Standards.

4.13 Loans with more than two applicants

For cases where there are three or more applicants you will need to complete two fact finds (one for the first two clients and then a further fact find for the third, and if applicable, the fourth client). You should then add some notes to cross-reference the two cases. All product(s) recommended, Reasons Why Letters and supporting documentation can be added/attached to just one of the two cases.

4.14 Guarantors

You must confirm in both the fact find and MRWL if this is a guarantor mortgage and that the guarantor has been advised to seek independent legal advice.

You must speak to the guarantor to satisfy yourself that they understand the implications of being named a guarantor and are happy to proceed. Guarantors must be given copies of the illustration and MRWL.

5. Designing Your Recommendation

5.1 General guidance

You must ensure your recommendation results in the right outcome for the customer.

It is not sufficient just to inform the customer of facts and risks (although you must do this as well). In addition, you must assess whether it is right that they take that risk and whether your recommendation is therefore the right outcome.

For example, if you recommend an interest only residential mortgage, it is not sufficient to only inform the customer that they need a clearly understood and credible repayment strategy and advise them of the associated risks. In addition, you should consider whether the customer should be taking this risk. If a repayment mortgage is affordable, given that the majority of repayment strategies are not guaranteed and/or may not be acceptable to the lender, this might be the better outcome for the customer.

Retaining comprehensive records of advice to demonstrate that you have understood your customer’s circumstances and that the customer is aware of all facts and risks is critical.

You cannot recommend a mortgage that is not affordable, even if it puts the customer in a better position than they are now. If customers are experiencing difficulties, they must be referred to their existing lender.

5.2 Lender criteria

All mortgage applications must meet the recommended lender’s published criteria; this includes obtaining, scrutinising and the submission of documentation required to support the application. If a lender agrees to transact outside criteria, your record must contain an email from the lender with details of what was agreed, who you spoke to and when.

If a lender rejects an application, you must fully record their reason for doing so in notes.

For BBTL customers who have multiple properties, you must obtain details of all properties and existing mortgages, to ensure the total number of properties meet lender criteria.

5.3 Affordability

You must ensure that your mortgage recommendation is affordable to the customer and your record of sale should reflect the customer discussions that support affordability.

The fact find must evidence that the customer can afford the mortgage recommended both during and after any initial benefit period. You cannot rely on old or historical information when assessing affordability.

You must take account of the impact of likely future interest rate increases on affordability for a minimum period of five years from commencement of the mortgage. Regardless of the fact that lenders must consider and factor in an interest rate increase, we would expect you to consider the impact and risk to the customer on affordability from a broader perspective.

If ‘benefits’ or ‘maintenance’ are being taken into account, the POS record needs to explain how the mortgage will remain affordable when they cease, if they’re of a nature where they’re likely to stop. For example, if they are linked to a child’s age.

For right to buy customers, you must ensure they are made fully aware of their responsibilities after completion, which previously would have been the responsibility of the Council. For freehold properties, this will include property repairs, maintenance and buildings insurance; for leasehold properties, this will include service charges and any known works of improvement for the first five years. Details should be included in the Council’s right to buy offer letter, which you should request from the customer. The cost of these must be reflected in the expenditure section and included in your affordability assessment.

Where reliance is placed upon non-salaried income such as investment income, you must make the customer aware that you are unable to provide advice in respect of these arrangements regarding the level of income, maturity values and level of guarantees, unless you are qualified and licensed to advise on such products.

You must satisfy yourself that your recommendation is affordable to the customer and allow funds to cover their protection needs.

The information obtained from customers and input to application forms must be accurate, and reflective of the information within the fact find, to enable an assessment of affordability to be carried out by lenders.

5.3.1 Affordability into retirement

Where the term extends beyond retirement, you must be satisfied that the mortgage payments will remain affordable, and the customer’s plans must meet the lender’s criteria.

This may be by way of pension, investment income or where one party remains in employment and will be able to afford the repayments on a sole income.

The source of the post retirement income must be recorded in the MRWL. If the source is pension or investments, you must not give advice on the potential return they may provide unless you are licensed in these areas. However, you must still have a discussion with the customer to establish whether they believe it to be feasible. If the customer is unsure of the return, you must recommend they seek independent advice.

Where the customer has some time to retirement and the lender requires evidence of post retirement income, copies must be attached to the POS record.

If the customer already receives pension or investment income as part or all of their income, then this must be evidenced and recorded and form part of the normal income and expenditure affordability assessment.

5.4 Loan amount

You must ensure that the customer only borrows the amount required to meet their current needs to avoid paying unnecessary interest. The POS record must be clear how you reached the amount applied for and include comprehensive notes of your discussions with the customer.

You may not know the exact amount as up to date figures have not yet been obtained for redeeming the existing mortgage or liabilities that are being consolidated. However, throughout the advice process sufficient information should be obtained to arrive at the amount needed prior to application.

For home movers wishing to retain equity, you must make the customer aware that they will pay interest on the extra amount over the term of the mortgage and by reducing the amount of borrowing, cheaper products may have been available.

You must consider ERCs as well as other costs to determine if it is suitable for the customer to switch lender or remain with their existing lender. If it is not suitable, you should recommend that the customer does not proceed.

Where a customer is looking to increase the borrowing already secured on the property, you must inform them that alternative finance options may be available and may be more appropriate, although you do not need to consider suitability of the options as listed below:

Obtain a further advance from their existing lender (unless the adviser knows that the existing lender will not provide further funds)

Re-mortgage to a new lender

A second charge mortgage

Unsecured lending

If the customer’s preference is to reduce their monthly outgoings by switching to a lower-rate product with a new lender, you should source the whole of market to check that this is financially suitable. You should explain that the customer is prepared to pay the ERCs to meet their objective and confirm why this is suitable in the MRWL. You must carry out a calculation to show the financial loss or gain in paying the ERCs upon switching to a new product (use fact find “notes”), and this figure must also be replayed to the customer and explained in your MRWL.

5.5 Consideration of existing assets

It is not a requirement to consider whether existing assets should be used to increase the deposit in order to reduce the mortgage amount. However, where applicable, you must point out to the customer the fact that realising such assets may reduce the overall cost of borrowing.

If the customer is able to provide a higher deposit that would result in a lower interest rate being available to them, this must be brought to their attention.

5.6 Repayment method

If the repayment strategy is credible and the customer fully understands the risks, e.g. if the intention is to downsize and there is sufficient equity to fulfill this plan, then it may be appropriate to recommend interest only or part and part. You must make the customer aware that they will need to demonstrate and evidence to the lender, and to yourself, all repayment strategies.

You will need to ensure that the repayment strategy meets the recommended lender's criteria and obtain evidence, as required by the lender and for record keeping purposes.

The following are examples of repayment strategies that are not permitted:

An inheritance that is expected but uncertain.

Reliance on house price increases.

Downsizing, where the lender considers there will be insufficient equity in the property to repay the loan and purchase another property mortgage free.

The above examples are not exhaustive and up to date knowledge of lender criteria is key.

Affordability may be assessed on an interest only basis where there is a credible repayment strategy. The cost of all repayment plans must be included as committed expenditure within the budget planner.

You cannot give investment advice unless you are licensed to do so. If your customer needs advice on investments, you must refer them to a financial adviser.

5.7 Debt consolidation

Debt consolidation should only be considered when you can evidence suitability and affordability and your record clearly shows the right customer outcome.

5.7.1 Debt consolidation – guidance for credit impaired customers

For customers who fall into the definition of credit impaired as per Section 4.9 and who wish to consolidate debts, you will need to assume that the debts will remain outstanding for the purposes of assessing affordability. You must therefore take this into account when making your recommendation. This also applies to further advances for credit impaired and debt consolidation purposes. You will need to make your customer aware that this may reduce the number of lenders and products available to them.

Debt consolidation should only be considered when you can evidence suitability and affordability and your record clearly shows the right customer outcome.

5.7.2 Debt consolidation – guidance for all other customers:

You must advise customers to speak to their existing lender or credit provider(s), prior to advising them on consolidation, as this may be cheaper.

You should investigate whether it would be more suitable to take a loan not secured on their property, and then refer them as necessary (see Section 2 for permissible referral activities)

If the customer has existing deposit based savings, you must recommend that the customer use the savings in the first instance, and where this is declined, document the reason for not using these funds

You must evidence all debts / loans / credit cards prior to any consolidation and attach copies to the record, or document why they could not be obtained and instead get the debt consolidation disclaimer signed by the client and added to the record.

If the customer has consolidated before and you deem it suitable to recommend further consolidation, you must explain why in the MRWL.

You must explain any potential consequences e.g. that if they continue to consolidate, their LTV increases and this can impact the deals available in the future in terms of rates and eligibility.

You must inform the customer of the potential overall cost of paying liabilities if they are consolidated to the mortgage. Whilst the mortgage interest rate may be lower, the term is likely to be longer and will increase the overall cost.

Many lenders will not permit debt consolidation on properties that have been bought under the right to buy scheme and are still within the right to buy period. You must therefore determine whether a property has been bought under the right to buy scheme and when this period ends.

Debt consolidation is not permitted in the following circumstances:

Short-term debts with less than 6 months to run

Small debts of less than £500

Interest free debts, unless the interest free period ends before drawdown of the mortgage and isn’t likely to be available again

Student Loans

The customer is consolidating debts to fund necessary expenditure, as this suggests affordability issues

Debts being consolidated to an interest only mortgage where there is no existing repayment strategy

Where a customer is consolidating debt and stretching term and switching part of the loan to interest only with no credible repayment strategy

The figures above are not limited to these minimums. In all instances you must consider the customer’s individual circumstances and exercise appropriate judgement. If you think it is unsuitable for the customer to consolidate debts, they must be referred to the Citizens Advice Bureau.

To ensure the customer is fully aware of the implications of consolidation, you must show the cost of consolidating debt by using either of the following options:

Produce, print and discuss two illustrations with/without the increased debt, or

Calculate the additional cost of borrowing by using the cost per £1 borrowed example in the Mortgage Illustration and multiplying by the additional amount required

5.8 Rejected advice

Where you cannot source a product that is suitable in meeting lender criteria and/or FPG minimum standards, you must inform your customer that you are unable to assist them.

If a customer rejects your advice, you cannot facilitate them proceeding with something else and you must not assist them, on any basis, in applying direct (i.e. what could be described as execution only).

5.9 Term

You should assess the customer’s situation to ensure that their needs, circumstances and preferences are considered to determine the term of the new mortgage. You should fully discuss with your customer the impact of future interest rate increases prior to recommending an appropriate term.

Rather than recommending the shortest term, it may be more suitable to recommend a term that allows the customer to overpay, which will reduce the overall term of the mortgage. If interest rates rise, the customer could then choose to stop the overpayments to ensure the mortgage remains affordable. This may be more practical, providing the customer is disciplined enough to make overpayments when funds allow. Your notes should contain all discussions held with the customer relating to the term of the mortgage, which should be reiterated in the MRWL.

5.10 Existing Lender Arrangements – General Guidance

Some existing residential mortgage lender arrangements include further advances, porting products (whether maintaining the existing loan or borrowing additional funds) and retention products/rate switches (where terms other than the interest rate remain unchanged, and this includes mortgage products that offer a feature which allows the customer to change rates at some time in the future, e.g. from tracker to fixed).

5.10.1 Further advances

Where a customer is looking to increase the borrowing already secured on the property, you must inform them that alternative finance options may be available and may be more appropriate, although you do not need to consider suitability of the options as listed below:

A second charge mortgage

Unsecured lending

If, after sourcing, the most suitable product is not recommended due to the customer’s overriding preference to stay with their existing lender, this must be fully explained in notes and the MRWL.

The Mortgage Illustration provided must be based on the amount of the further advance only.

5.10.2 Rate switches and ported products

For existing lender products such as rate switch and ported products where the illustration has to be obtained direct from the lender, you must attach a copy to the POS record.

5.10.3 Evidencing suitability and affordability

Where the advice is to stay with the existing lender, FPG requirements for evidencing affordability may be reduced where the following requirements can be satisfied

The adviser or AR Firm gave the initial advice

The adviser confirms with the customer there have been no significant changes in lifestyle, income or expenditure

The new mortgage payments are not higher than the lenders SVR.

In the instances where the above criteria is met, the evidence can be reduced to a minimum of the following

Evidencing that no mortgage payments have been missed in the last twelve months,

A recent bank statement showing the income going in and the expenditure going out, as this will confirm affordability and that the customer’s income has not reduced since the last advice,

If a BTL, then you must also obtain evidence that any profits are declared to HMRC for taxation

Notes must be added to the POS record to confirm the above criteria have been met. Lender requirements must also be adhered to.

Please note that where there is an increase in payment then affordability must be fully assessed using 3 months’ bank statements, as with any other residential mortgage.

6. Sourcing a Mortgage

6.1 General guidance

Advisers must ensure that they undertake sufficient fact finding before sourcing products and should apply their market knowledge and professional judgement alongside the output from sourcing systems.

You cannot access a lender or special rate through any other mortgage club or network other than the Legal & General Mortgage Club or a FPG approved packager.

You must ensure that your mortgage sourcing system, Mortgage Brain, is always updated with the current release of lender deals.

You must disclose to the customer the impact that filtering their preferences will have, e.g. this will mean that some products are removed, which may or may not be cheaper.

You must attach a product list to the POS record for every recommendation. If you’re aware of a more suitable product that doesn’t appear on the sourcing list, the list must still be stored to support that your recommendation is more suitable / cheaper and a Mortgage Illustration should be obtained direct from the lender and attached to the POS record.

The FCA’s rules around Cheapest Deal mean that we require you to retain your sourcing results, ordered by total to pay in the introductory period, or by the FCA total pay. These results must be attached to the POS record to show that the mortgage recommended is the cheapest for the customer. Sourcing filters must match the fact find preferences, with any discrepancies fully explained in the fact find “notes”.

Where more than one mortgage contract is identified as being suitable, the least expensive should be recommended, taking into account other features that may be important to the customer e.g. quality of service or level of arrangement fees.

If the recommended product is not the top of the product list, notes must be added to the POS record explaining why the product(s) higher up the list weren’t suitable. For example, the customer doesn’t comply with lender criteria and why. This explanation should be replayed to the customer in your MRWL.

If the loan was not available via the sourcing system, your POS record must include a detailed explanation of why a particular lender/bank has been recommended, whether any other lending institutions were considered and, if so, why these were not progressed.

6.2 Ensuring your customer is eligible

Sourcing results may include products that the customer is not eligible for. It is your responsibility to ensure that the customer is eligible for the mortgage recommended according to the published lender criteria at the time of application. Where you utilise a ‘criteria tool’ or an ‘affordability tool’ then you can attach this system output to your POS to help evidence your justification of lender.

7. Mortgage Illustrations

7.1 General guidance

In most instances a lender should provide an ESIS (European Standardised Information Sheet), however in certain circumstances a KFI may still be produced. The phrase “Mortgage Illustration” will be used in these standards and refer to the KFI or ESIS.

Where the customer only wants a general idea of what is available in the marketplace or an idea of cost before you have commenced the full advice process, you cannot provide a “Mortgage Illustration” which shows that ‘advice and recommendation’ has been given. Therefore, you must make it clear that you are providing generic information only, and not advice.

A Mortgage Illustration must be provided to customers in the following circumstances:

  • If you advise the customer to enter into or vary a regulated MCOB or CONC mortgage contract with debt consolidation.
  • On payment of a product related fee (e.g. valuation or booking fee).
  • Before an application is submitted.

It is an FCA requirement that before an application is made, you must give the Mortgage Illustration to the customer and provide an adequate explanation of each section, and what this means to them.

When submitting the mortgage application, you must inform the chosen lender of any broker fee payable by the customer, in order that the lender can correctly calculate the APRC which will appear in the offer letter.

Care should be taken to ensure all Mortgage Illustrations are correct. Details should match those contained in the mortgage offer letter (which should be checked against the Mortgage Illustration) and any discrepancies investigated with the lender.

The Mortgage Illustration should show the date that the document was produced.

It is a regulatory requirement that the POS record must show the following:

The date that the Mortgage Illustration was provided to the customer, and

The date the application was submitted, and

Details of how the Mortgage Illustration was provided (e.g. face to face, email, post etc.)

7.2 ESIS Section 4 (or KFI Section 8)

Where you charge a fee as a percentage of the loan, this must be converted in to £’s in the fees section of the Mortgage Illustration (to show the actual fee charged).

For example, your Disclosure Documentation discloses that you charge 1% of the loan. For £100,000 this means you will be able to charge up to £1,000. Therefore, it is the £1,000 figure (not the 1%) that must be disclosed within the appropriate section of the Mortgage Illustration (and your fee agreement).

In all cases, the Mortgage Illustration must only be obtained from the sourcing system or direct from the lender.

7.3 ESIS Section 4 (or KFI Section 13)

It is a requirement that the procuration fee details within the illustrations shows that this will be payable to FPG and the AR Firm, if applicable.

7.4 Reduction in property value post submission

If a property is down valued post submission, you must check that the new mortgage is still the most suitable and affordable.

If you believe the product applied for is still the most appropriate, you should attach a copy of the illustration showing the new loan and purchase price/house value to the POS record.

If the product is no longer available, or if you think a different product is more suitable, a new product list and illustration should be attached.

You must also issue either a new MRWL, or a MRWL addendum, for the revised recommendation. A new MRWL will be required where the customer’s circumstances have changed sufficiently for the original advice to be re-visited, for instance a property and lender change (so a completely new transaction) or it could be that the client has separated, got a new partner, had a child, changed jobs or taken on a financial commitment. However, where the change is not sufficiently significant for the advice to be re-visited, for instance a change in borrowing amount, or lender, or product, then you may prefer to issue a MRWL addendum instead. In both situations, you are required to confirm that it is still affordable during and post any initial benefit period.

Where the customer asks you to challenge the valuer’s decision then this can be done, however you must first obtain any required comparables of recently sold properties, or other evidence, and you must also make this appeal via the lender, and not to the valuer directly. Where a lender does not accept appeals to valuations then no challenge can be made. It is important that no contact is made with the valuer as we cannot be seen to be influencing their decision.

7.5 Re-issuing an illustration

If there is a change to the product terms or an increase/decrease in the loan of more than 5% after the last illustration was given to the customer, the advice must be re-visited and a new illustration issued.

If the change is less than 5% and the product terms remain the same, you do not have to provide a new illustration unless requested by the customer, as this will be confirmed in the lender’s offer letter. Notes should be added to explain the change.

7.6 Manual Mortgage Illustration

The sourcing system will not always support the production of Mortgage Illustrations, and where this is the case then a Mortgage Illustration should be obtained from the lender directly and attached to the POS record. A manual Mortgage Illustration will need to be added with the key information in order to produce a MRWL and update the case status. Sourcing results (which will not include your recommended product) are still required in these instances.

8. Mortgage Reasons Why Letter

8.1 When to issue

You must give the customer a MRWL for all regulated mortgage advice within ten working days of making the mortgage application, or by exchange of contracts if this occurs earlier.

Where an application has been made but does not proceed to completion, a MRWL must still be issued as advice has been given.

If customers reside at different addresses, you must send a MRWL to each customer.

With your customer’s consent, you must also issue a MRWL to the guarantor(s), recommending that they seek independent legal advice.

8.2 When to re-issue

You will need to issue the MRWL Addendum letter, or if you prefer actually re-issue the MRWL if, after submission of the application:

The mortgage increases by more than 5% of the original amount

There is a material change, an explained in Section 7.4 of these Advice Standards

In addition, you must re-visit your advice to check that the product is still the best available.

For changes of less than 5% of the borrowing amount, or for changes concerning the lender, product, or term, etc, a MRWL Addendum letter can be sent to the customer, confirming the changes and the reasons why. A copy should be attached to your POS record.

If the lender has increased the recommended term, the MRWL should be amended and re-issued, or an addendum letter sent, explaining that the lender will only accept the application on a longer term. This means that the overall cost of the mortgage will be greater if the loan is kept to full term. Where overpayments can be made, this should be pointed out to the customer as an option to reducing the increased term.

If the mortgage decreases by more than 5%, everything else remains the same and your recommendation is still the best advice, then you do not need to re-issue the MRWL.

When completing the MRWL, you should ensure it is customer specific, summarises their circumstances, preferences, and objectives, and clearly details how your recommendation provides the right customer outcome.

8.3 MRWL Key Points

The following describes the fundamental key points that should be covered in the MRWL where applicable:

  • If you’ve recommended a product transfer or retention product, you must explain why this is suitable, e.g. it may be due to the customer’s circumstances that another lender will not lend, or it’s the customer’s preference to remain with their existing lender.
  • Why you have recommended a particular lender and confirmation that from those suitable it’s the least expensive from those available. If not, a clear explanation must be given in the MRWL as to why, such as the customer’s overriding preferences. If the more expensive recommended product is preference-based rather than criteria-based, e.g. the client is eligible for the cheaper product, then the difference in cost must be replayed to the customer in the MRWL.
  • Confirmation that the customer accepts your recommendation and that you have checked they meet the lender’s criteria before submission of the application.
  • Where the customer is moving home and retaining equity, the reason why and the relevant risk warnings.
  • If you have recommended a switch of lender, including where ERCs are applicable, you must explain why this was more suitable than staying with their existing lender, having taken into account all associated costs. Your calculation of the difference in cost must be retained in the fact find “notes” and you can also include this calculation in the MRWL if you wish.
  • Why you have recommended a specific repayment method. For all or part interest only, you also need to detail how the customer intends to repay the loan, together with the associated risk warnings. Your MRWL should contain sufficient personalisation to evidence feasibility/plausibility, plus the reasons for your interest only recommendation.
  • Why you have recommended a specific mortgage term, including the ages at which the customers intend to retire.
  • Why you have recommended an impaired credit mortgage, together with the risk warning that these may not be the most competitive products.
  • Why you have recommended the interest rate type, any initial benefit period or end date, and why this is suitable. You should add any applicable risk warnings. Note - if you inform a client you will contact them before the end of their initial rate benefit period, you must ensure that you do so.
  • Where the initial benefit period is less than five years, confirmation that the implications and costs of re-broking have been fully discussed.
  • Whether you have recommended an offset facility and any associated risk warning.
  • Where the property is to be rented out, the MRWL should confirm all the risk warnings under a BTL heading.

8.3.1 Affordability

You must confirm the loan is affordable and the basis on which you have assessed this.

You must detail:

Full details of all income and the evidence provided to support this.

That you have concluded that the mortgage is affordable both during and post benefit period.

Any expected changes to the customer’s circumstances that support affordability.

If the mortgage term extends beyond intended retirement, then how the mortgage payments will remain affordable must be confirmed.

For Government HTB type equity schemes with deferred interest payments, confirmation that the increased payments from year six will be affordable.

Confirmation as to how the Government shared equity loan will be repaid.

8.3.2 Mortgage Term

You must detail why you have recommended a specific term and include the discussions held with your client in relation to the impact of future interest rates rises on affordability.

Where the lender has stretched the term you must explain this. Where the customer has the ability to make overpayments, this should be detailed as this will enable the customer to reduce the term of the loan, as well as the overall cost.

Whether a split term arrangement has been recommended and why.

For mortgages with a split term, whilst the interest only element may match the term of the repayment strategy, for the repayment element the shortest term must be recommended unless there is an overriding preference. Both terms must be explained within the MRWL.

Whether you’ve recommended that all or part of the mortgage term extends beyond intended retirement, and if so, why this has been done and how payments will remain affordable.

8.4 Other information that must be included within the MRWL, where applicable:

8.4.1 Debt consolidation

You need to confirm what has been consolidated and why this is suitable.

In the MRWL you must:

Record your recommendation for the debt consolidation and the customer’s chosen course of action, together with the impact.

Explain why debt consolidation is suitable. In particular:

If the customer has consolidated before, you must fully explain why you believe it is suitable for them to do so again. For example, the previous consolidation related to one-off costs for home improvements.

Where the customer has a reasonable level of disposable income and has no real need to consolidate for financial reasons – the letter needs to explicitly explain why they want to consolidate.

Include risk warnings:

That the overall cost will be higher

Adding unsecured debts to the mortgage means that they now become secured on their property

Consolidating debts will increase the loan to value which can impact future deals in terms of rates and eligibility

Confirm in the MRWL you have illustrated the cost and impact of consolidating by providing the customer with two Mortgage Illustrations, or where you have calculated the cost of consolidation, include the figures within the MRWL. For example:

If the Mortgage Illustration shows the cost per £1 is £2.23 and the loan is £100,000 including consolidation of £10,000, the calculation would be as follows:

Cost per £1 is £2.23.

Total cost of £100,000 over term will be £223,000.

Cost of £10,000 will be £22,300.

Total cost of loan, excluding debt consolidation, will be £200,700.

Where the customer’s existing liabilities are in arrears, you must advise them to speak to their creditors before proceeding as they may be prepared to come to an arrangement that the customer could consider as an alternative to adding these to the mortgage.

Refer to Section 5.7.2 of these Standard for circumstances when any debts should not be added to the longer term mortgage debt.

8.4.2 Joint review but mortgage application in one name

Where you complete a joint review but recommend that only one customer applies for the mortgage, you must document in the MRWL why this is suitable and how it is affordable, with the risk that the customer will be solely responsible for repayments.

8.4.3 Multiple mortgage sales

Where the record contains recommendations for more than one property, you may include all recommendations within the same MRWL if you prefer, although you will need to ensure that each mortgage, with its own set of preferences, is addressed appropriately. The property addresses and respective loan amounts (including any fees added) should be listed on the MRWL. Alternatively, you can of course simply create a new MRWL for each different property purchase.

8.4.4 Adding fees to the mortgage

Where your customer has chosen to add fees to the loan this must be confirmed in the MRWL, with the risk warning that interest will be charged on these throughout the term of the mortgage.

Where applicable, the MRWL must also confirm the following:

The reason why existing funds are not being utilised

If the customer intends to add fees and pay them within a short period after completion

It should also be pointed out that some fees may not be refundable if the mortgage does not proceed.

8.4.5 Bridging loans

You will need to confirm that you have not given advice on any associated bridging loan and that the customer should seek independent legal advice from a solicitor to ensure they understand any contract they enter into with regard to funding the purchase, or capital raising needs, if they have not already done so.

8.4.6 Builders Incentive Schemes / Government Shared Equity Schemes

You must remind the customer of the risks associated with the above schemes and confirm these risks in the MRWL.

The amount you ultimately repay to the builder/Government Scheme organisation will depend on the value of the property at the time of repaying the loan.

Any improvements you make to the property may increase the value of the builder’s/Government Scheme organisation’s equity share as well as yours.

If the property has to be valued for any reason, you may be liable for the cost of this valuation.

I have not given you advice in respect of the second charge.

I have given you advice on the mortgage contract only. You will need to seek independent legal advice from your solicitor to ensure that you understand any such contract you enter into in respect of funding the purchase.

When you sell your home, you must repay any scheme assistance from the sale proceeds.

You should ensure that you have a separate repayment arrangement in place to repay this facility.

We have discussed and considered whether you are able to afford to purchase a property using your own resources, rather than by borrowing under a shared equity credit agreement, but you are unable to raise the additional funds required for the type of property you require.

8.5 Buy To Let Mortgages

You must inform the customer of the risks associated with BTL properties and confirm these risks in the MRWL.

  • That it is your intention to let the above property via an Assured Shorthold Tenancy, or similar tenancy agreement, and that it is not intended for use as a residence by you or your family
  • I am unable to advise you on whether property is a good investment.
  • The property may command a lower rent than anticipated.
  • Tenants may default on payments or leave the property so you could suffer loss or reduced income.
  • Property may be untenanted for a period of time and the customer may then have to support two or more mortgages.
  • Being liable for the maintenance of the property and responsibility to insure.
  • Professional advice should be sought on legal, tax, financial and property management issues.
  • The property value may fall as well as rise.
  • property falling into negative equity.

You are responsible, if applicable, for complying with the Houses in Multiple Occupation regulations, which have made it mandatory for landlords to obtain a license for Houses in Multiple Occupation.

9. Record Keeping

9.1 General guidance

FCA record keeping Rules require a record of all advice given, not just cases that complete. A POS system record must be completed for all regulated business transactions, and contain all information obtained and advice given to the customer, including Decisions in Principle.

Throughout these standards we require specific information to be documented in either fact find notes or the Mortgage Reasons Why Letters (MRWL). You should strive to ensure your notes contain anything relevant to your recommendations, or material to your advice that impacts a particular preference the customer may have. For example, why the term of the mortgage has been recommended and the discussions around this.

You must update the record as to the accurate status of the case - e.g. submitted to provider, exchanged, not taken up or rejected by the lender as and when the event occurs.

If a case does not proceed to completion, the reason why must be recorded e.g. customer found another property, pulled out of sale, unsatisfactory survey, etc. Where the lender has declined an application, you must fully record their reason for doing so in notes.

Records must be retained for six years after the business relationship ends. (Mortgage term plus six years unless it is known that the mortgage has redeemed before the end of the term).

If paper records are obtained, they must all be scanned and attached to the case. Where paper records are retained, these must be securely stored and retrievable within two business days.

Appendix 1 – Lifetime Mortgages

This document is to be read in conjunction with the Mortgage Advice Standards.

Definition MCD Lifetime Mortgage

A MCD Lifetime Mortgage is where:

  • Clients are retired, or
  • Clients aren't retired, but will be during the mortgage term, and
  • The lender will seek full or partial repayment of the loan (and interest if rolled up) until one or more of the specified life events occur

The specified life events are:

  • The death of the client
  • The client leaves the mortgaged land to live elsewhere and has no reasonable prospect of returning (e.g. moving into residential care)
  • The client acquires another property for use as their main residence
  • The client sells the mortgaged land
  • The lender exercises its legal right to take possession of the mortgaged land under the terms of the contract

General General Guidance

You cannot advise on Lifetime Mortgages without first having obtained the required industry qualification (ER1) and then having had your licence granted by the Compliance Director.

All relevant Mortgage Advice Standards apply to lifetime mortgages, including debt consolidation.

You cannot source lifetime mortgages via the Mortgage Brain sourcing system, but you can use The Exchange to evidence your sourcing, and you will then need to obtain all KFIs (mortgage illustrations) direct from the lender (as these mortgages are exempt from MCD regulation, the KFI will apply rather than ESIS).

Lifetime Mortgage advice is subject to 100% checking of cases and you should notify the compliance department whenever you advise on a Lifetime Mortgage product. Any remedial work from the 'case check' will need to be signed-off as satisfactory before a mortgage can complete.

Disclosure Disclosure

A Lifetime Mortgage specific disclosure document must be provided to a client confirming that your advice is limited to lifetime mortgages only, and not home reversion plans. Bespoke disclosure documentation is provided by the Compliance Department.

Fact Find Fact Find

The Key (point of sale software) should be used to record your Lifetime Mortgage advice – adhering to the same minimum requirements as for any normal residential mortgage.

4.1 Equity Release Mortgage Questionnaire

Advisers are expected to complete an Equity Release Mortgage Questionnaire as part of the fact find. Specific attention should be paid to the state of health of the clients because ill health can have a positive impact on the borrowing potential. The purpose of the loan must be clearly established in respect of whether a lump sum, drawdown, or combination product is most suitable. All other avenues to raise capital must be explored before resorting to lifetime mortgage advice.

Risks Risks and Future Needs

You must discuss and document in the MRWL the following risks of releasing equity:

  • Potential risks of losing the home – the client can live in the property as long as they comply with the terms and conditions, e.g. lender permission may be needed before allowing others to occupy the property.
  • If the client doesn't comply, the lender has the right to request the amount owed and the client may lose their home.
  • The impact of increases or decreases in house prices against the growing debt, e.g. loss of equity.
  • The effect of death or long-term care on respective partners – where one of a couple dies, household income may reduce. The mortgage must be repaid on the death of the last survivor or if they move into long-term care.
  • The effect if the client wants to move – a lifetime mortgage can be transferred subject to the lender's criteria, but moving to a lower value property may require partial repayment.
  • The potential impact on their estate – interest rolled up will reduce the amount left to the estate. The client should seek appropriate advice.
  • The expectation that the client maintains the property – if they don't, the lender may insist on repairs and charge this to the client.
  • The possibility of a further advance in the future – interest rates at the time of a further advance may differ, further reducing the estate.

Suitability Additional Suitability Requirements

If retirement is less than one year away for one or both clients, you must recommend they seek advice on retirement provision options. If declined, the MRWL must document this and note the risks. If the client is within four months of retirement with pension projections available, the Turn 2 Us software can be used based on these projections.

MTBs Means Tested Benefits

For all Lifetime Mortgages, the client's eligibility for MTBs and the impact of releasing equity must be assessed using the Turn 2 Us software. Results must be attached to the client record in The Key. The key MTBs that may be impacted are:

  • Pension Credit
  • Council Tax Reduction
  • Universal Tax Credits
  • Health Benefits

Releasing equity could also affect eligibility for Local Authority Grants and Social Funds if means tested in the future.

ERC Equity Release Council

It is recommended that lenders for Lifetime Mortgages should be members of the ERC. See www.equityreleasecouncil.com. If recommending a non-ERC member lender, the MRWL must include a standard paragraph indicating this, an explanation of the ERC and the protection it offers, and the consequences of not having an ERC-marked product.

Uses Ways To Release Equity

FPG does not permit equity release to be used for:

  • Investment purposes
  • Purchasing a BTL where repayment is on death
  • Inheritance tax planning
  • Significant funds placed on deposit for a later non-specific use

Money for 'rainy day or emergency funds' is allowed on a case-specific basis. We would not expect this to exceed £10,000. Any figure in excess requires explanation. If the money raised is to be gifted to family, the client must be advised to seek legal and/or financial advice. The MRWL must confirm whether they declined or proceeded based on advice received.

Where a drawdown facility is included, the MRWL must make clear this does not form part of the recommendation unless a clear future need is identified.

Alternatives Alternatives To Lifetime Mortgages

You must consider and document whether other methods of releasing funds may be more suitable. Alternatives to consider:

  • Local Authority grants for essential repairs (e.g. leaking roofs, windows)
  • Use of existing savings
  • Home reversion schemes (see below)

Reversion Home Reversion Schemes

FPG Advisers are not permitted to advise on Home Reversion Plans. If a home reversion scheme is considered most appropriate, you cannot give advice or make a recommendation — document this in notes. Home reversion may be appropriate where:

  • The client wishes to guarantee leaving a percentage of the property's equity to their estate
  • The client has a need to raise more funds than a lifetime mortgage will allow
  • The client's attitude to ownership is not an issue, as ownership transfers to the product provider

Equity Protecting Equity In The Property

Where the client has a desire to protect part of the equity in their property, a product that has this guarantee must be recommended, if available.

Existing Existing Loans and Mortgages

Where the client wishes to remortgage to increase borrowing, make them aware it may be more appropriate to take a further advance with their existing lender rather than move providers. Any remortgage to a more favourable rate must account for the additional setup costs and any early redemption penalties in the affordability assessment.

Legal Legal Advice or Family Involvement

You must:

  • Recommend that the client seeks independent legal advice and explain why
  • Recommend that the client consult with their family to manage expectations
  • Document these discussions in the MRWL and confirm whether the client involved their family
  • Give the client adequate time to seek advice. Where declined, document this in the MRWL.
  • Document in the fact find how family involvement is to be managed

POA Power of Attorney

When dealing with a client's attorney, the fact find must confirm the power of attorney has been granted and include the attorney's name. A copy of the Power of Attorney must be obtained and attached to the record. Identification must be obtained for both client and attorney. The MRWL should be in the name and address of the attorney with the client's name and address in the heading. Further guidance: gov.uk/power-of-attorney.

MRWLs Mortgage Reason Why Letters

Specific lifetime MRWL letters are available in the POS library. Each must be client-specific and cover key risks. Use the correct MRWL for the product recommended:

  • Lifetime – where interest is rolled up
  • Lifetime – where some or all interest payments may be made
  • Interest only

MRWL letter 2 must be used if the product may at any time include features of both interest only and roll up, or interest payments are made and it is deemed to be a lifetime mortgage by the lender.

For debt consolidation cases, the lifetime mortgage MRWL must also explain: the interest rate applied may be higher than existing borrowings; as the term is likely extended, overall cost will increase; for roll-up loans, interest is charged and rolled onto capital, increasing the debt.

Wills Wills

You must recommend that your client reviews their Will arrangements. Where the client hasn't made a Will, you must recommend that they do so.

1. Introduction & Overview

The protection and general insurance advice standards reflect the FCA and Finance Planning Group (FPG) requirements for advising on protection policies. It encourages the application of customer centric behaviours as defined in FCA handbooks Treating Customers Fairly handbook (TCF), and the wider principles (PRIN).

1.1 Requirements

You, as an adviser of FPG or of a FPG Network firm, have a regulatory responsibility at all times to:

Act in the customer's best interests when giving advice to ensure a good customer outcome is achieved

Ensure that the documentation given to the customer confirms your advice in the clearest way

Follow these standards

APERs within AR firms and FPG as a Network have a regulatory responsibility to ensure that you follow these standards.

You must always encourage your customers to review all of their protection needs.

Where no specific ruling or guidance exists within these standards, we expect that an individual or AR firm will take an intelligent view, and one that is in the interest of the customer, and not seek to exploit any apparent omissions.

These Advice Standards are aimed at Multi Tie Advisers (Sole Tie Adviser should instead refer to comments in brackets where necessary).

1.2 Permitted Activities

FCA ICOBs Regulated products

You are only permitted to give advice on and sell the following suite of insurance products if the appropriate licences are held.

  • Term Assurance
  • Critical illness cover
  • Family Income Benefit
  • Income protection
  • Accident, sickness and/or unemployment
  • Buildings and contents
  • Landlords Insurance
  • Whole of Life
  • Relevant Life Plans
  • Business Protection
  • Over 50s Plan
  • Met Life products

1.3 Self-sales

Self-sales are not permitted without authorisation from your Sales Manager prior to any application. Where such sales are made you will also be required to sign a “no advice” declaration, as will your partner if applicable, and this should be added to the POS (Point of Sale system, which is Acre).

2. Customer Meetings

2.1 Initial Disclosure

You must provide your customer with a Disclosure Documentation on first contact when you anticipate making a personal recommendation on a protection policy. You must attach this disclosure document to the customers POS record. You must verbally go through all sections of the disclosure document with your customer. The IDD version in Acre does not cover-off us keeping in contact with clients in the future, and so it is mandatory to discuss the FPG version of the Disclosure Document and GDPR form with each customer. Best practice is for the customer to sign the document too, but this is not essential where your customer has signed a previous version of the form.

2.2 Factfinding

When introducing the fact find, you must stress to the customer the importance of receiving full, honest and accurate answers to the questions you ask them. Equally you should ensure that you ask for the information required by questioning in a way designed to encourage the customer to give you all the information you require honestly and accurately.

If you are advising more than one customer, you must speak to each one to gather the information you require, or at least speak to both customers to confirm the accuracy of the information that has been provided if by another.

The fact find must be fully completed in all areas relevant to the scope of service disclosed.

Where you’ve given disclosure documentation to your customer indicating that you all be giving mortgage and protection advice, you must ensure that you then finalise the advice process by confirming your recommendations for both mortgage and protection.

If your process is to separate the protection advice (e.g. one adviser deals with the Mortgage advice, and another adviser deals with the Protection advice) then the initial adviser must include a note in the Mortgage Suitability Report to explain that a second adviser will deal with the protection aspect of the advice. On handing-off the case to the next adviser, the initial adviser should also pass over copies of the customer’s personal and address ID, to save having to repeat this process with the client. Any aspects of customer vulnerability must also be mentioned, for the same reason.

Existing Policies

All existing policies, employer benefits (including sick pay or death in service), or provisions that could be used to cover an event (savings etc.) must be recorded in your fact find in order to get a full picture of the customer’s circumstances.

You should request copies of a customer’s existing policy/ies (that relate to your scope of service) in order that you can get accurate details.

You must endeavour to gain all of the following details of a customer’s existing policies and employer benefits including:

Life/lives assured and how the policies are written

Type of cover

Current sum assured/benefit payable (e.g. waiver, guaranteed insurability options etc.)

Start and end date of the policy

Monthly cost of each policy, which should also be included in the budget planner where these are to continue

You should obtain sufficient details of existing policies (including employer benefits) so you can advise on, or include them, in your recommendation. Whether the client has evidenced these policies/benefits, or merely told you about them, should be recorded in the Protection Suitability Report.

You should seek the customer’s views on their existing policies and record the discussion in your POS record:

What were/are they intended for?

Do they intend on keeping them and why?

Would they want to replace them and why?

You should decide whether using current policy flexibility options or any guaranteed insurability options that may be available could result in a better outcome for the customer than a replacement policy. You should note in your POS record the reasons for or against using these options, including the difference in cost and/or benefits.

Where a client doesn’t provide you with sufficient information on their existing policies and / or their employment benefits you cannot advise on their suitability and should exclude these from your recommendations and record this in your Protection Suitability Report. Where a client doesn't know what their entitlement to sick pay is under their contract of employment you should recommend an income protection policy with a short deferred period.

Affordability

A full budget section must be completed in all instances EXCEPT if buildings and/or contents insurance is the only protection being discussed with the customer. Just a summary of income and outgoings documented in the expenditure section is acceptable for B&C only. Neither payslips nor bank statements are required though.

The customer must have sufficient available funds to be able to afford your recommendation.

Mortgage & Protection (or protection to cover a mortgage event) = Future mortgage payment must be included and consideration given to any increase in bills. This should be added to current expenditure to determine available funds.

You must recommend within a client’s disposable income, but not to any limited budget figure they have provided. Where the customer’s disposable income does not allow you to make a full/ideal recommendation, then you should reduce the cover being recommended accordingly. Conversely, where there is ample disposable income to meet your recommendation, you must make that recommendation even where the customer is telling you they wish to pay less. Within the Acre POS, the “Budget for Protection” should be the customer’s disposable income figure. Having made your ideal recommendation, where this exceeds the customer’s desired premium payment, you can then discuss options with the customer to help them achieve their chosen solution to partially cover their agreed need areas.

Protection Requirements (formerly Aims and Views)

It is mandatory that you attempt to discuss with a customer all of their protection requirements. If you are able to have this discussion then the Health & Protection Needs section of Protection Sourcing must be completed with the client. Then you must fully complete the Requirements fields in the Protection Sourcing section to reflect the customer needs, personal views, reasons for wanting or not wanting protection and the financial impact of not protecting themselves. Your Protection Suitability Report should confirm the Objectives boxes of the Requirements section back to the customer and so please take care to type full, grammatically correct, sentences which will be understood by the customer.

You have to make your customers fully aware of the possible financial consequences of them not being adequately protected.

You can produce indicative illustrations for the customer during your discussions so as to give a guide on potential premiums. Indicative quotations do not form advice and so a Protection Suitability Report is not needed.

Once the customer’s priorities have been expressed you can then provide recommendations and personalised illustrations accordingly within affordability.

The areas of protection that need to be discussed with your customer are:

  • Income protection – Permanent Cover and short term Temporary Cover
  • Critical illness cover for the mortgage
  • Life cover for the mortgage
  • Critical illness cover for family/lifestyle
  • Life cover for family/lifestyle
  • Buildings and Contents Insurance
  • Landlords Insurance

Unemployment Cover – You must always consider the customer’s potential entitlement to redundancy pay from their employer prior to making a recommendation for unemployment. You must discuss with your customer the risk that any company can cease trading or restructure and the resulting discussions should help you form a view as to whether there is a need. It’s important that you document in the POS record any such discussions.

Other ancillary products – We have further products available, which do not current sit within a standard product framework of Term, CIC and IP. These products are from the likes of One Family with their Over 50s Plan , and from Met Life with their Mortgage Safe, Everyday Protect and Child Safe products. Whilst each of these products might have merit for discussion and recommendation in certain instances, their discussion is not mandatory. However, where you do discuss these products and make a subsequent recommendation, then this must follow the process for a fully advised sale. (Please refer to Appendix 6 for more information on One Family’s Over 50s Plan, and to Appendix 7 for more information on Met Life’s three products.)

3. General Rules of Recommendation

Your recommendation must result in the right outcome for the customer. Your POS record and Protection Suitability Report must contain adequate information and explanation in order for FPG or regulatory bodies to understand the reason why your recommendation was suitable and how it met the needs of the customer.

When sourcing a protection product, you are required to do this through the POS (Acre) or you can manually source outside of Acre using either the Assureweb or Solution Builder sourcing systems. If you externally/manually source protection, ensure your required quotes and sourcing results are saved back to your POS record manually. (Sole Tie Advisers should manually save and upload all quotes to the POS record too, but there will be no sourcing list of differing insurers.)

PRIOR to giving your recommendations, through discussions with your customer, you should ensure that their current circumstances will allow them to take out the policies that you are recommending. (For example, you should not recommend a CIC policy and find out on completing an application form that the client has had a major illness previously).

You should also ensure that they do not have plans for the future that would affect their chances of a claim being successful. (For example, at the time of application the customer knows that they intend on working overseas).

To assist you in matching your customer with an appropriate insurer where medical disclosures, or other unique circumstances, are presented by the customer, you may use the Underwrite Me tool to help with that application process, or you may contact the Protection Helpdesk for a steer. In instances of extreme poor health, you may refer your customer to Cura who are a separate Protection Advice Firm who may be able to secure your customer with some cover from one of the specialist insurers on their bespoke panel.

You must always give the customer the latest Key Features Documentation, a statement of price (personal illustration) and an Insurance Product Information Sheet (IPID) for any GI sale. Also, any necessary supporting documentation to support your recommendation, in writing, prior to submission of any application(s) to the protection product provider. This also applies to any protection product the customer wishes to proceed with outside of your recommendations, for instance their differing chosen solution.

You must verbally disclose to the customer the content of the relevant product Key Features Document and statement of price.

Existing policies

If sufficient existing policy details have been received, you must make a recommendation to the customer as to whether they should retain or cancel each policy. It’s not acceptable to sell a new policy and leave it for the customer to decide whether to cancel or retain.

You must discuss what the customer values about the benefits within their existing policy e.g. illnesses, definitions, benefits. These discussions will help establish if the current policy will provide enhanced cover or benefits for the customer. You must also establish if the customer will be subject to any costs because of cancellation e.g. a deferred mortgage fee that may be invoked, or the ability to claim a mid-term refund of a general insurance premium.

You must ensure that you have fully compared policy benefits between the existing cover and proposed cover prior to deciding on whether to recommend cancellation.

If the existing policy fully covers a current customer need, you would normally recommend retention. Conversely, if the existing policy does not fit the need better than a new policy, then cancelling might be suitable.

You must not rebroke a policy unless it is in the best interests of the customer (not the adviser). The reasons for re-broking must be fully explained in the Protection Suitability Report.

You cannot make a recommendation to retain or cancel if you are only giving advice to one party on a joint life policy. You must speak to both parties, so if you are giving advice to only one then you must include a statement in the existing policy section of the Protection Suitability Report that you have been unable to advise both parties and that you have not taken the policy into account when giving advice.

If your recommendations include cancellation of an existing policy, you must advise the customer to not cancel the existing policy until any new policy is on risk.

You should only recommend the cancellation of a CIC policy if, when comparing to the new cover, the customer is unconcerned that they might lose cover for a particular illness (or alter their ability to claim) in comparison to their current cover. For all CIC cancellations you must first obtain a CI Expert report and any definitions or illnesses being lost must be explained to the client. This must be done with the customer before you advise on cancelling or retaining that existing CIC policy, and it must then be attached to the POS record.

If you cannot access a customer’s existing 3rd party policy documents, then you must make it clear that you have not taken them into account during your recommendation and confirm this in the Protection Suitability Report.

Conversely, retention may only be suitable if the existing policy terms are superior to that of the policies currently available.

Where a type of cover or benefit is lost, or the ability to claim is affected because the cover gained from a replacement policy results in a more suitable policy overall, then any such loss of cover or benefit, or loss of ability to claim must be fully discussed and agreed with the customer before proceeding, and this must be documented in the Protection Suitability Report.

Where an employed customer has existing Death-In-Service cover provided by their employer, then this type of cover should not be considered to meet their mortgage protection needs, as it is considered temporary cover because it is lost if the employee changes employer. You should advise the client that this type of cover is meant for family protection purposes only. Where you have a client who insistent on utilising their DIS to cover their mortgage, then their precise comments should be recorded in your POS record.

4. Policy Suitability & Recommendations

A decreasing term policy must be recommended for a repayment mortgage.

For decreasing policies the standard “policy assumed interest rate” used should be 8%, however, you can reduce this rate to as low as 5%. This rate is important to ensure there is always sufficient cover on a decreasing policy to repay a capital and repayment mortgage. Reducing the rate will have the effect of lowering the monthly premium. You can only increase the “policy assumed interest rate” where you are protecting a loan with an interest rate greater than 8%.

A level term policy must be recommended for an interest only mortgage.

CIC to protect a joint mortgage > recommend our preferred CIC provider joint life policy (or two single life CIC policies if this is preferred by the client, in which case please highlight the difference in cost in your Protection Advice Letter) with guaranteed premiums and sum assured to match the mortgage amount including fees that have been added to the loan, and a term to match the mortgage term. (Sole Tie Advisers should recommend the most comprehensive product from those available from your insurer, where applicable).

CIC to protect a single mortgage > recommend our preferred CIC provider single life policy with guaranteed premiums and sum assured to match the mortgage amount including fees that have been added to the loan, and a term to match the mortgage term. (Sole Tie Advisers should recommend the most comprehensive product from those available from your insurer, where applicable).

Where an individual has a preference for serious illness cover over critical illness cover, then please refer to Appendix 1 for the process you are required to follow.

Life cover to protect a joint mortgage > recommend the cheapest joint policy payable first death, (or two single life policies if this is preferred by the client, in which case please highlight the difference in cost in your Protection Advice Letter), with a sum assured to match the mortgage amount plus any fees that have been added to the loan, and a term to match the mortgage term.

Life cover to protect a single mortgage > recommend the cheapest single life policy with a sum assured to match the mortgage amount plus any fees that have been added to the loan, and a term to match the mortgage term. You must have a clear reason in your factfind aims and views as to why a single person wants life cover and record those in your Protection Suitability Report.

Shared equity loans may be better covered by a separate LTA policy for the amount and term of the equity loan. Where no term for the equity exists, then link it to the term of the mortgage. You must discuss with the client the merits of either level cover or index-linked cover to protect the equity loan as this loan will fluctuate with changes in the property price.

For shared ownership mortgages it would be more suitable to recommend an LTA (and CIC if applicable) for the maximum share that can be purchased, less any deposit being provided by the customer. The mortgage can then be repaid and any surplus can be used to fund rental payments or buy all or some of the remaining share. You must discuss with the client the merits of either level cover or index-linked cover to protect the rented element of the property value as this element will fluctuate with changes in the property price.

Single life policies may be more suitable than joint life but only when:

Age restrictions on a policy stop you from recommending the full term to both customers

Where customers are arranging their mortgage as tenants in common

Customers consider that their future needs may be better served by the single policies (for example, where flexibility may be required. Note – you will be required to specify in your Protection Suitability Report what flexibility it is that the customers require, and why)

CIC is arranged as there is now a feeling within the industry that individuals having their own CIC policies might better serve their needs, given that in most instances a CIC payout is not being used to repay a mortgage debt.

If recommending Life cover to protect any other form of liability, full details of that liability will need to be included in your finalised fact find, held in the POS system.

Where you refer a customer to a second charge mortgage provider and you establish that they have a protection shortfall prior to the loan commencing, then you must make your protection recommendation immediately. Additionally, the client will then have a need to protect the second charge mortgage being arranged, once it commences.

Protection of a family or the lifestyle of the customer

You must recommend protecting the customer or their family in the event of death or critical illness where the customer has indicated this area of protection as one of their priorities. Again, life cover only should be recommended based on price, whereas critical illness cover should be recommended based on our preferred CIC provider at the time.

Taking into account continuing existing cover, employee benefits, or any other provisions or resources to be used to reduce the cover required, the cover recommended should then be:

To protect a family - life cover and/or critical illness cover providing a monthly benefit in the event of death, sufficient to cover the need, for the term required

To protect a Lifestyle need - life cover and/or critical illness cover to provide a lump sum or monthly benefit, sufficient to cover the need, for the term required

You will need to establish the amount of cover required with your customer, and that discussion is likely to be focused on providing sufficient cover to replace a salary, covering the outgoings of the household, adapting the home to a change in circumstances, or the cost of replacing the essential duties of an individual. The required term is likely to provide cover until the youngest dependant will be self-sufficient or until all dependants cease to be financially reliant on the customer.

A summary of this discussion and the reason for the amount and the term must be documented in the Protection Suitability Report.

Where relevant, the following factors should be taken into account when establishing the amount of cover required:

Existing savings and investments

Costs that may cease during the term of the cover e.g. mortgage

Other income streams that may commence during the term e.g. widow’s pensions, retirement income

The customers aspirations for their future lifestyle

The age and the needs of dependants

Protection of an income

When considering the amount of cover needed, each customer’s situation may be different and should be treated as such.

You must firstly discuss the income that will be required in the event of long-term sickness or unemployment. Taking into account continuing existing cover, employee benefits, or any other provisions or resources to be used to reduce the cover required, the cover recommended should then be:

Standard IP or a combination of standard IP/AS (or standard IP plus Met Life’s Mortgage Safe) to cover a period of sickness, until the customer intends to retire, covering the need in the most effective way, for the amount of income required to cover their need (not just the monthly mortgage repayment amount).

To utilise stepped benefits for any period where payment is received from employers, continuing income from an employer or existing/new ASU/IP policies

Cover for unemployment, where that need is agreed to be addressed.

Income Protection must be recommended to your customer’s intended retirement age, and not the term of their mortgage.

You must stress the importance of reviewing cover on a regular basis to ensure the cover and the term are appropriate to the customers need.

Prior to making your recommendation you must decide the most effective and suitable solution to cover the need. This will be established in discussion with the customer and considering the following:

The individual’s occupation type which may affect the recommended cover/insurer

What deferred period is most suitable (and whether stepped benefits can provide the best outcome)

What product or combination of products/options best meets the customer’s needs (cost isn’t necessarily the measure of this)

Whether the customer can fund a period of sickness themselves

Standard IP will be a more suitable recommendation than the low cost (1 year, 2 year, 5 year) alternatives

BTDO cover on ASU could be more suitable than standard cover

  • The inclusion of Fracture Cover in your solution where this is available
  • Where Met Life products are discussed, that these are not to be recommended in place of IP (unless perhaps the client is ineligible for IP), as the Met Life products only offer temporary cover, and there are restrictions on when some benefits are payable

Rules for when recommending the Vitality Serious Illness Cover (SIC) product

This version of the Vitality advice standards replaces the previous version issued in February 2017, and is being introduced following the release of the Vitality Mortgage Plan. Where a client wishes to take out a policy that covers a range of medical conditions that are not covered under a standard critical illness cover policy advisers must recommend the Vitality Mortgage Plan.

Whilst serious illness cover (SIC) is similar to a conventional critical illness policy (CIC) there are a number of differences between the two. SIC covers a significantly larger number of medical conditions, but the basis of the cover is different for some of them. In a ‘standard’ CIC contract, if any of the claims criteria are met, the policy will pay out in full. With the Mortgage Plan 77 core conditions are guaranteed to pay out 100% of the sum assured, whilst with other conditions the amount received may be less than 100% depending on the cover provided for that condition, or the severity of it. Vitality have therefore included Serious Illness Cover Booster within the product, and it is not available as an optional extra, and cannot be excluded.

With CIC, if a successful claim is made, the sum assured is paid out, and the policy ends. With SIC, if a claim is met with a partial pay out, cover will remain for the difference between the amount paid, and the amount of cover in the policy immediately before the claim was made. If the severity of the condition increases, the client may be able to make further claims on the policy, up to the maximum permissible for the condition concerned.

One of the main attractions of Vitality for clients is the arrangement they have that gives special offers from their business partners. With the Mortgage Plan this cannot be added as an optional extra to a level premium contract. In view of this, if a client wants to include the option, advisers must recommend clients include the Optimiser discount as this will give them access to these benefits.

The Optimiser discount gives clients an up-front discount on their premiums based on the assumption that they will engage fully with Vitality’s Healthy Rewards Programme. Should they not do so their premiums will increase over time. Details of the potential increases in premiums are included in the illustrations produced from either SolutionBuilder/Assureweb, or the Vitality portal.

If a client does not want to have access to the special offers, when creating a quote, advisers should select the Essentials option as this will give access to the level premium contract.

  • The definition of a Relevant Life Plan

A Relevant Life Plan (RLP) is a term assurance plan available to employers to provide an individual death in service benefit for an employee’s family and financial dependants. It is designed to pay a lump sum if the person covered dies or is diagnosed with a terminal illness whilst employed during the term. The employer pays for the Relevant Life Plan.

Relevant Life Plans aim to provide a tax efficient benefit provided by an employer for an employee. They are designed to comply with the conditions for Relevant Life Policies contained in section 393B(4) of the Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003. In order for the payment of a RLP premium to be considered as allowable as a deductible business expense the usually “wholly and exclusively” principles apply. As a result, the RLP should not be for use as a business protection policy (e.g. key person assurance, shareholder protection).

Appendix 4 – Business Protection

  • Business Protection

This document is to be read in conjunction with the Protection Advice Standards.

  • The definition of Business Protection

Business Protection is an umbrella term covering three different financial areas that may need cover. These are in respect of Partners or Director’s Share Protection, Key Person insurance, and Commercial Debt insurance.

Partners or Director’s Share Protection

In the event of the death of an owner in a business it is likely his (or her) beneficiaries will want to withdraw their capital from it. Similarly, from the remaining owners’ perspective it is unlikely they will want the deceased’s next of kin actively involved in the business. Each owner will therefore take out insurance in trust for the estimated value of their holding in the company. They should also take out a Share Purchase Agreement which will detail how shares can be bought and sold. This document, along with the trust means that in the event of any owner’s death their holding in the business can be bought by the remaining owners.

Key Person Insurance

Key Person insurance will protect a business against the financial impact of the serious illness, or death, of a vital employee. For instance, where the relationship between the business and important clients would be jeopardised by the loss of the individual, it can also lead to potential financial loss. But in fact a Key Person within a business could just as likely be someone instrumental in the running of the business, as it could be for a successful salesperson bringing a high level of revenue. Key Person insurance would replace this lost income, or perhaps fund the replacement of any lost skills or knowledge.

Business Loan Insurance

With business loan insurance, owners, partners or directors can insure any outstanding debt, and in the event of their death the debt could be repaid. It can be taken out to cover any of the following:-

  • Overdrafts.
  • Commercial loans.
  • Commercial mortgages.
  • Directors’ loans.

assurance plan available to employers to provide an individual death in service benefit for an

employee’s family and financial dependants. It is designed to pay a lump sum if the person

covered dies or is diagnosed with a terminal illness whilst employed during the term.

For Partnership or Shareholders Protection the recommendation should be to cover the full value of each individual’s holding in the business. In some instances, it may also cover what needs to happen in the event of the death of a director, or owner.

For Limited Liability Partnerships (LLP’s) you need to see the partnership agreement as this contains similar information to the Articles of Association. It sets out the rights and responsibilities of each partner, plus the distribution of any profits or debts. It can also include future eventualities (such as death or retirement of a partner, and adding new partners etc.). Please note whilst it is a legal requirement for LLP’s to have a partnership agreement, it is not for a traditional partnership.

It is also important that you recommend the partners/directors set up a Share Purchase Agreement as this is a legal document detailing how the shares of the business are to be bought and sold by the other partners/directors.

The most common form of Share Purchase Agreement is a Cross Option Agreement, and a number of the insurers on our panel can provide draft documents. These may be provided to businesses for reference purposes, but you should always recommend they obtain legal advice on the agreements, and have these documents prepared by their solicitors.

7. Premium Equalisation

The cost of protection for each of the parties will differ depending on their age, state of health etc. Where one of the partners or directors is significantly older than the others the cost for any given amount of cover will be higher than for the younger ones. The individual could then argue he is paying more than the others, and is less likely to benefit from any claim. To offset this, we can apply a measure of Premium Equalisation, and Legal & General have provided a calculator to enable us to do this.

It is not mandatory to recommend equalizing premiums, but be aware that if the company is paying the premiums on behalf of the individuals, it is treated as a taxable benefit in kind by the Inland Revenue. Therefore, if one individual’s premiums are much higher than the others it will reduce their personal allowance.

  • Whole of Life

This document is to be read in conjunction with the Protection Advice Standards.

  • The definition of a Whole of Life policy

An alternative to Term Assurance, [here as the name implies] the Whole of Life cover has no end date. Whole of Life is similar to other types of life cover in respect of the additional benefits:-

  • Terminal Illness
  • Indexation
  • Waiver
  • General Guidance

Our preferred option for life cover is to recommend Term Assurance for mortgage related protection, and family income benefit for family protection. There are occasions however when neither of these products will fit the client’s needs, and therefore Whole of Life may be the appropriate solution. The occasions when this may apply are as follows:-

Funeral Costs

This type of cover is currently available from a number of providers on a non-advised basis (Over 50’s cover). However, for commission to be payable advice must be given to the client.

Legacy

Typically, this is where a client wishes to leave a specific amount of money to a nominated individual, or individuals (e.g. a grandparent wanting to leave money to a grandchild).

Partner/Spousal Support

The type of situation where this may be appropriate is if a client knows their spouse or partner is financially dependent on them and wants to leave them a lump sum when they die.

Adult Dependents

If a client has an adult dependent (e.g. a physically or mentally handicapped child) they may want to leave a lump sum to provide care for them when the client dies. In these situations, it is the client’s decision on how much cover is required, and this will often be premium driven.

Mortgage Protection

There are an increasing number of clients who have interest only mortgages, with no repayment vehicle, and are approaching retirement. These clients may be able to continue to pay the interest on the mortgage from retirement income, without the need to transfer to an Equity Release product. In these situations, where the term of the product is open-ended, any associated life cover needs to be as well.

Revolving Credit

In situations where it is possible a client will continue with borrowing (e.g. a credit card) and does not plan to pay it off when retired, it may be appropriate to recommend whole of life to repay the debt on death.

For both mortgage protection and revolving credit the adviser should recommend cover based on the current, or anticipated balance outstanding.

Appendix 7 – Met Life Products

  • Met Life’s 3 unique products:
  • Mortgage Safe
  • Everyday Protect
  • Child Shield

This document is to be read in conjunction with the Protection Advice Standards.

  • The definition of these 3 very different Met Life products

Mortgage Safe – This is a type of Pure Protection policy, a bit like a type of Mortgage Payment Insurance (MPI) which can cover the clients mortgage payments only. There is a non-underwritten accident option, or a simplified underwritten product providing accident and sickness cover.

Everyday Protect – This is a product which pays a small lump sum for the likes of broken bones or hospitalisation.

Child Shield – This product is very similar to the Everyday Protect product, but provides cover for children.

  • General Guidance

Our preferred option to meet the needs of clients is to recommend Term Assurance, Critical Illness Cover or Income Protection based on the individual needs of the client. This is because the cover for those fully underwritten contracts will provide the client with a good outcome based on their agreed needs. Although we do not expect these Met Life products to be recommended in place of the aforementioned traditional products (Life, CIC, IP), we feel there is a place for the Met Life products as there may be instances where your client is uninsurable through the fully underwritten route, or where the client expresses an interest in protecting themselves against accidents, broken bones and hospital stays, etc.

The ‘need areas’ where we believe these Met Life products may be suitable are as follows:-

Mortgage Safe

This product is suitable for clients looking to protect their monthly mortgage repayments in case they are unable to work due to accident and/or illness. The monthly benefit is used to pay the mortgage for either 12 or 24 months, after a 4 week deferred period, and up to £1,500 per month benefit. The accident only option has no underwriting, whereas the accident and sickness option has simple underwriting with just a few qualifying questions being asked. To define an ‘accident’ it is a sudden event which happens by chance and couldn’t be expected. Either option could be recommended after your client has been declined or heavily rated by an insurer offering normal Income Protection.

Everyday Protect

This product is suitable for clients looking to protect themselves or their dependents in the case of accidental injury or hospitalisation. The lump benefits can be used to supplement a reduced or lost income, for example. The amount of benefit is determined by the number of blocks of cover the client wishes to pay for. And whilst this cover is not a replacement for critical illness cover, it can be recommended irrespective of whether the client already has critical illness cover, or not. This product is not underwritten as the basis for cover is accident-based. It could be suitable for anyone really, but in particular those who do not have sick pay, or who are unable to obtain critical illness cover, or those in more manual jobs or who play sports, and so are perhaps more susceptible to injury.

Child Shield

This product is suitable for clients looking for financial protection if their child suffers a broken bone, is hospitalised, or is diagnosed with a specified serious condition. A lump sum of money is paid out to help with additional expenses. This product can be recommended instead of Children’s Critical Illness Cover (which is sold as an add-on to CIC) where your client prefers the broader benefits offered by this Child Shield product.
